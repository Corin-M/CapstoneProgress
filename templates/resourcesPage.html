What are Social Stories?

Social stories are traditionally written and illustrated first- or third-person stories that inform the reader of the future events, expectations, rules, and potential triggers of the experience described by the story. Autism educator Carol Gray developed a very strict story-telling format she named the Social StoryTM in the early 1990s. In her words “A Social Story accurately describes a context, skill, achievement, or concept according to 10 defining criteria (Gray nd). Despite this strict definition, the term social story has expanded considerably in use within neurodiverse communities. Most commonly, it is used to describe any short narrative preview with some illustrated/visual component intended to prepare the reader for a new experience or to shape behavior in a particular context (Rymanowicz 2015).

As digital media has been explored more as a potential teaching tool, social stories have evolved further to include video walkthroughs that stay in the form of a story. Videos are found to be more effective tools for individuals with autism as they allow for the transfer of relevant sensory information such as ambient noises and bright lights while better capturing the relevant actions modeled (Ayres 2005).

It is important to note that social stories are not meant to coerce individuals into specific behaviors or thought patterns. For example, a story that pushes the general narrative of “I will sit still whenever someone is talking” suppresses the healthy habit of stimming. Using social stories that focus on modifying or suppressing behaviors to be more socially “acceptable” or “normal”, has been found to be harmful to individuals and perpetuate ableism by putting the weight of being accepted onto the learner (Vance 2020). However, when used correctly social stories are an excellent tool to help an individual set clear expectations about future experiences and grow confidence in their abilities.

When are social stories useful

Social stories are most useful when they are created with a clear purpose in mind and follow a couple of key requirements.

  • Social Stories should be made as specific as possible, working best when a specific user is kept in mind or even helps create them.
  • Social stories should be mostly descriptive not prescriptive. Explaining what will happen and what the environment is like, over forcing specific behaviors for the user.
  • Video social stories that show a lot of visual and other sensory information make them far more effective previews. If you do a written story, be sure to include relevant sensory information in your story.
  • Traditionally, social stories are most useful for previewing changes to routines, complex interactions such as medical appointments or entertainment events, and growing independence.

Where can I learn more about social stories?

The number one official resource on Social Stories is Carol Gray’s Website which has a wide variety of example written stories and a free membership that gives access to workshops and official guidelines.

An additional useful resource for example social stories is the collection from Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports World Social Story website. This list has both an overview of social stories and a long list of additional resources and practice stories.

An overview of research on social stories up until March 2022 can be found in the meta-analysis "Autism Spectrum Disorder and Social Story Research: a Scoping Study of Published, Peer-Reviewed Literature Reviews" conducted by L. Camilleri, K. Maras, and M. Brosnan.
